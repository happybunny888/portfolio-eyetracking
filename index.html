Bakery Menu

On a day out, it is always important to get energy from snacks while you explore!

Eye Tracking Project Description:
Eye Tracking is a common method for evaluating the performance of a webpage.
For this project, my group members and I were required to redesign two versions of the given bakery homepage, make the redesigns in Figma, and then bring two participants to conduct eye tracking tests.



Design A
This is our Design A (the "aesthetic version") as it has less text, more elegant font, more images, more space for the menu.
Main portion: The bakery name, hours of operation, location, menu category labels, and images.
Sidebar: Bakery shop logo and navigation for the rest of the bakery site.

Design B
This is our Design B (the "informative version") as it has more text, more simple font, and more menu options.
Main portion: The bakery name and paragraphs about the bakery.
Side of Main Portion: Broad menu categories and options within each category.
Sidebar: Bakery shop logo, navigation of site, hours of operation, and location.



Hypotheses

As a group, we noted our hypotheses based on the two versions, about how the differences affect where a user’s eyes are drawn?

Null Hypothesis

  • There will be no significant difference between eye movement between the two versions.

Alternative Hypothesis

  1. For the aesthetic version (Version A), people’s eyes will be drawn more toward the menu options, while for the informative version (Version B), people’s eyes will be drawn toward the explanatory paragraphs.

Testing

Heatmaps (a still image that depicts where our participants’ gazes were fixed) for each design were generated.

Heatmap for Design A
Heatmap for Design B

Analysis

  • In the informative version (B), users spent more time on the menu (both the website navigation and the actual food menu), while somehow missing the drink options.
  • In the aesthetic version (A), the user’s eyes were drawn more towards the bottom of the screen, whereas the user’s eyes were drawn more towards the top in the informative version (B).
  • In the aesthetic version (A), the user did not look too closely at the menu images, but rather at the captions that described what they were (e.g. “cakes”, “pastries”).
Conclusion:
It is important to design your website by keeping in mind how users' eyes will move across the page.
